The Logistic Bandit Problem

Mar 27, 2026 · Why a decade-old conjecture was hard, and how I resolved it

This post gives the intuition behind my paper “An Information-Theoretic Analysis of Thompson Sampling for Logistic Bandit Problems”, published in TMLR (02/2026). The paper resolves an open question that had been explicitly stated as a conjecture by Dong & Van Roy in 2018, and which traces back to a challenge posed by McMahan & Streeter in 2012: can we prove that Thompson Sampling behaves well on logistic bandits without paying an exponential price in the slope parameter?

The setup

In the logistic bandit, at each round you pick an action a from some set, and receive a binary reward (click or no click, success or failure) with probability given by the logistic function of the inner product ⟨a, θ⟩:

$$P(\text{reward} = 1) = \frac{e^{\sigma \langle a,\, \theta \rangle}}{1 + e^{\sigma \langle a,\, \theta \rangle}}$$

The parameter $\theta$ is unknown; $\sigma > 0$ is a known slope. Both the actions and the parameter live in the $d$-dimensional unit ball. The goal is to learn which action is best while minimising cumulative regret, the total reward lost compared to always playing the optimal arm.

This is a natural model for click-through rate prediction, personalised recommendation, and clinical trials with binary outcomes. The logistic function is everywhere in practice. So why was the theory stuck?

The slope problem

Every prior bound for this problem, whether using UCB or Thompson Sampling, scaled exponentially with the slope $\sigma$. The best frequentist result (Faury et al., 2020) had a leading-order term of order $d\sqrt{T} \log T$ (already good), but a lower-order term that grew as $e^\sigma$. All Bayesian bounds were worse, with the exponential appearing in the dominant term.

This is frustrating because empirically, larger $\sigma$ makes the problem easier. When $\sigma$ is large, the logistic function is steep: small differences in $\langle a, \theta \rangle$ translate to large differences in reward probability, so near-optimal actions are easy to identify. The bounds were running in exactly the wrong direction.

Dong & Van Roy (2018) made this precise by conjecturing that the Thompson Sampling information ratio for logistic bandits should be bounded by a constant times d, independent of σ and of the number of actions. Their own analysis could not prove it, and direct application of their linear bandit techniques produced bounds that explode exponentially in σ. The conjecture sat open for six years.

Where the exponential comes from

To understand the difficulty, recall how the information ratio is defined: it is the squared expected regret divided by the mutual information gained about the optimal action. Bounding it requires upper-bounding the squared regret and lower-bounding the information gain.

The information gain comes from the reward signal, which is a Bernoulli random variable. To lower-bound how much a Bernoulli outcome tells you about the parameter, the standard approach links it to the variance of the reward probability: if the probability is uncertain (high variance), observing the outcome is informative.

The regret, on the other hand, is the gap in expected reward $\mu_\sigma(\langle A^*, \theta \rangle) - \mu_\sigma(\langle A, \theta \rangle)$, where $\mu_\sigma$ is the logistic function with slope $\sigma$. Previous analyses bounded this using the expectation of a function of the inner product gap. The problem is that when $\sigma$ is large, the logistic function saturates: near-zero inner product gaps lead to near-zero regret, but the function linking them has slope proportional to $\sigma$ near the transition point and slope approaching zero elsewhere. Controlling the ratio of regret to information gain via the expectation forces you to account for the worst-case slope, which introduces a factor of $\sigma$, and iterating this argument compounds into an exponential.

The two key ideas

The proof of the main result, an information ratio bounded by $d(4/\kappa)^2$ where $\kappa$ is a geometric constant measuring how well the action set covers the parameter space, rests on two innovations.

Variance instead of expectation. Rather than upper-bounding the squared expected regret by the square of the expected regret (a standard step), the analysis bounds it via the variance of the regret. This is made precise through a general lemma: if $f(x)/x$ is non-decreasing (a condition the logistic gap function nearly satisfies, up to a correction), then $\mathbb{E}[f(U)]^2 / \mathbb{E}[U]^2 \leq \mathrm{Var}[f(U)] / \mathrm{Var}[U]$. The information gain is then lower-bounded by twice the variance of the reward probability via a data-processing argument. This lets both sides of the ratio be expressed in terms of variances, and the $\sigma$-dependent slopes cancel.

Taking $\sigma \to \infty$ as a uniform bound. Even after switching to the variance formulation, the ratio of variances across the regret surrogate and the reward probability function still depends on $\sigma$ in principle. The second innovation is to show that this ratio is monotone increasing in $\sigma$, so its supremum over all $\sigma \geq 0$ is achieved in the limit $\sigma \to \infty$, where the logistic function becomes a step function and the ratio is bounded by a pure geometric constant (at most 4, independent of everything). This is the step that makes the bound uniform.

The result

Combining the variance bound on the information ratio with a quantisation argument to handle continuous parameter spaces, the main theorem gives:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Regret}(T)] \leq \frac{4d}{\kappa} \sqrt{T \cdot \log\!\left(3 + 6\kappa\sqrt{\frac{T}{2d}}\right)}$$

The dependence on $\sigma$ is only logarithmic, matching the intuition that larger $\sigma$ makes the problem easier. The bound is independent of the cardinality of the action space, so it applies to continuous action sets. And it resolves the conjecture of Dong & Van Roy: the information ratio is indeed $O(d)$, with no fragility dimension needed.

The constant $\kappa = \min_{\theta \in \mathcal{O}} \max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \langle a, \theta \rangle$ measures the worst-case alignment between the action set and the parameter space. When the action space contains the parameter space ($\kappa = 1$), the bound simplifies to $O(d\sqrt{T} \log(\sigma T/d))$, matching the minimax lower bound for linear bandits up to logarithmic factors.

What this means in practice

The numerical experiments in the paper confirm the theory: as $\sigma$ grows, the empirical regret of Thompson Sampling decreases, while all prior bounds increase exponentially and quickly become vacuous. The new bound tracks the empirical regret throughout.

More broadly, this result helps explain why practitioners have long found Thompson Sampling to work reliably across logistic bandit settings, including large or continuous action spaces, even in regimes where theory offered no guarantees. The theory was just behind the practice.

Paper: Amaury Gouverneur, Tobias J. Oechtering, Mikael Skoglund. “An Information-Theoretic Analysis of Thompson Sampling for Logistic Bandit Problems.” Transactions on Machine Learning Research, 02/2026. OpenReview · arXiv:2412.02861

What’s next

The analysis technique, using variance rather than expectation to control the information ratio, is not specific to the logistic link function. The key properties exploited (smoothness of the link function, the Bregman divergence structure of exponential family KL divergences) are shared by the full class of generalised linear bandits. Extending the results there is a natural next step, as is connecting to the frequentist setting via the Optimistic Information Directed Sampling framework of Neu, Papini, and Schwartz (2024).
