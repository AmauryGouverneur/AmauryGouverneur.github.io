What are Bandits, and Why Does Thompson Sampling Work?

Mar 27, 2026 · A non-technical introduction to the explore–exploit tradeoff

A recurring theme in my research is the question of how an agent should balance learning and acting when it cannot do both at the same time. This post is an attempt to explain that question, and one beautiful answer to it, without assuming any background beyond curiosity.

The slot machine problem

Imagine you walk into a casino and sit down in front of a row of slot machines. Each machine has its own (unknown) average payout. You get to pull arms one at a time, observe the result, and keep the winnings. Your goal is to make as much money as possible over, say, one hundred pulls.

The difficulty is immediately apparent. To know which machine is best, you need to try them. But every pull spent on an inferior machine is money you could have made on the best one. This is the explore–exploit dilemma: gather information, or use the information you already have?

This toy problem, the multi-armed bandit, is the simplest formal model of sequential decision-making under uncertainty. Despite its simplicity, it captures the essence of a surprisingly large class of real problems: a recommendation system deciding which article to show a user; a drug trial allocating patients to treatments; an optimizer tuning hyperparameters of a model. In all of these, every decision is both an action (exploit what you know) and an experiment (learn something new).

Measuring performance: regret

How do we judge an algorithm’s performance? The standard answer is regret: the total reward lost by not always pulling the best arm. If you knew which arm was best from the start, you would pull it every time and collect the maximum possible reward. Regret is the gap between that ideal and what your algorithm actually achieved.

Good algorithms achieve regret that grows slowly, ideally as the square root of the number of pulls, or $O(\sqrt{T})$, rather than linearly. A linear rate means you never really learned; a $\sqrt{T}$ rate means you identified the best arm quickly and then exploited it. The goal of bandit theory is to design and analyse algorithms that achieve this, and to understand the limits of what is achievable.

A naive approach, and why it fails

One instinct is to be greedy: always pull the arm that currently looks best based on what you have observed so far. The problem is that greediness exploits too aggressively. If arm A pays off on the first pull and arm B does not, a greedy algorithm abandons B forever, even if B is actually far better and just got unlucky once.

The opposite extreme is to explore uniformly: spend a fixed fraction of pulls on each arm regardless of what you learn. This wastes too many pulls on arms you have already identified as poor.

Effective algorithms balance these extremes. The classic approach, UCB (Upper Confidence Bound), adds an exploration bonus to each arm’s estimated value: the less an arm has been tried, the larger its bonus. This makes under-explored arms artificially attractive, guaranteeing that every arm gets tried enough. UCB works well and has clean theoretical guarantees, but it is somewhat mechanical. Thompson Sampling takes a more elegant probabilistic approach.

Thompson Sampling: acting as if you believe

Thompson Sampling was proposed by William Thompson in 1933 and largely forgotten until it was rediscovered empirically around 2011.1 Its simplicity is remarkable.

At each step, instead of computing a point estimate of each arm’s value, Thompson Sampling maintains a full posterior distribution over what each arm’s true payout might be. This distribution encodes both what you believe and how uncertain you are. Then, to choose an action, it does something simple: sample a plausible world from the posterior, and act optimally in that world.

More concretely: if you believe arm A has a 70% chance of being the best arm and arm B has a 30% chance, Thompson Sampling will pull A roughly 70% of the time and B roughly 30% of the time. This is neither greedy (which would always pull A) nor uniform (which would split 50/50). The more certain you are about which arm is best, the more you exploit it; the more uncertain you are, the more you keep exploring.

This is exploration as a byproduct of uncertainty, not as a forced intervention. The algorithm is just doing what a rational Bayesian agent would do: act according to what is plausible.

Why does it work? The information-theoretic view

Thompson Sampling has good empirical performance, but why does it work theoretically? The cleanest answer comes from an information-theoretic framework developed by Russo and Van Roy.2 My own research builds directly on this framework.

The key idea is to ask: at each step, how much does the algorithm learn relative to how much it loses? Define the information ratio as

$$\Psi_t = \frac{(\text{expected instantaneous regret})^2}{\text{information gained about the best arm}}$$

If this ratio is small, the algorithm is learning efficiently: it only incurs regret when it is genuinely uncertain, and each time it takes a suboptimal action, it learns something. Russo and Van Roy showed that if the information ratio is bounded by some constant $\Gamma$, then total Bayesian regret over $T$ steps is at most

$$\sqrt{\Gamma \cdot T \cdot H(A^*)}$$

where $H(A^*)$ is the prior entropy of the optimal arm, essentially how uncertain you were at the start about which arm was best. The more uncertain you are, the more time you need; but if $\Gamma$ is small, you learn efficiently throughout.

The remarkable fact is that Thompson Sampling has a provably bounded information ratio. The intuition is elegant: because Thompson Sampling samples its action from the same distribution as the optimal arm, the action it takes is always plausibly optimal. When it is suboptimal, it must have been genuinely uncertain, and that uncertainty is precisely what gets resolved by the observed reward. Regret and information gain are therefore tightly coupled, and neither can happen without the other.

From simple bandits to richer settings

The bandit model I have described is deliberately bare: a fixed set of arms, stationary reward distributions, no side information. Real problems are messier. My research has been about extending the information-theoretic analysis to settings where the simple version breaks down.

Linear bandits. When actions are feature vectors and rewards are linear functions of an unknown parameter, the number of “arms” is effectively infinite (or continuous). Standard entropy-based analysis cannot handle this because the entropy of a continuous random variable is unbounded. My work with Borja Rodríguez-Gálvez and our supervisors shows that by approximating the optimal action at a finite resolution, and carefully controlling the approximation error, one can still obtain near-optimal regret bounds for Thompson Sampling in continuous action spaces, recovering the classical $O(d\sqrt{T} \log T)$ rate in $d$-dimensional problems and extending it beyond finite action sets for the first time.

Papers A & B — “An Information-Theoretic Analysis of Thompson Sampling with Infinite Action Spaces” (ICASSP 2025) and “Chained Information-Theoretic Bounds and Tight Regret Rate for Linear Bandit Problems” (under revision, IEEE Trans. Information Theory; ICML 2024 Workshop).

Contextual bandits. When a context (e.g., a user profile) is observed before each decision and the optimal action depends on it, the standard information ratio is no longer the right object to analyse. There is a natural generalisation, the lifted information ratio, that conditions regret and information gain on the observed context. My paper at ISIT 2023 extends the lifted analysis to general sub-Gaussian rewards, generalising earlier work that only covered binary outcomes, and provides simplified proofs using classical information-theoretic tools.

Paper C — “Thompson Sampling Regret Bounds for Contextual Bandits with sub-Gaussian Rewards” (ISIT 2023).

Logistic bandits. When rewards are binary and the success probability follows a logistic function of the arm–parameter inner product, previous bounds scaled exponentially in the logistic slope parameter $\beta$. This was widely known to be an artefact of the analysis, but resolving it had remained open for over a decade. My solo paper derives the first bounds that depend only logarithmically on $\beta$ and are independent of the number of actions, by exploiting the variance of the reward rather than just its expectation.

Paper D — “An Information-Theoretic Analysis of Thompson Sampling for Logistic Bandit Problems” (TMLR 2026; NeurIPS 2024 Workshop on Bayesian Decision-Making under Uncertainty). arXiv:2412.02861.

The bigger picture

Bandits are a microcosm of a broader challenge: how should a rational agent under uncertainty allocate attention between learning and acting? The information-theoretic lens makes this precise. Regret is the cost of not knowing; information gain is the benefit of exploring. An algorithm with a bounded information ratio is one that never spends more than it learns, it is efficient in a deep sense.

This perspective extends beyond bandits. In reinforcement learning, where actions affect future states and not just immediate rewards, a similar trade-off governs performance. My thesis explores several steps in this direction, including Bayesian regret bounds for Markov decision processes and parameter-free PAC-Bayesian guarantees for offline learning. The bandit case remains the cleanest place to see the ideas, but the principles carry over.

If you want to go deeper, the best starting points are the tutorial by Russo, Van Roy, Kazerouni, Osband, and Wen on Thompson Sampling, and the monograph Bandit Algorithms by Lattimore and Szepesvári. For the information-theoretic analysis specifically, the key paper is Russo and Van Roy (JMLR 2016).
